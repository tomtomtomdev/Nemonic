# Reminiscences of a Stock Operator – Trading Skill

A framework for applying Jesse Livermore's principles (as captured by Edwin
Lefèvre) to real trading decisions, market reading, and trader self-discipline.

---

## Core Philosophy

Livermore's entire method rests on a few foundational ideas. Internalize these
before applying any tactical guidance:

1. **The market is always right.** Your opinion is worth nothing against the
   tape. The price *is* the truth.
2. **Time is as important as price.** The best traders wait. Most losses come
   from impatience, not from being wrong about direction.
3. **Losses are tuition.** Every loss teaches something. The traders who blow
   up are those who fail to pay attention to the lesson.
4. **Speculation is a business.** It requires study, patience, and discipline —
   not luck, tips, or hunches.
5. **The big money is made in the big swings.** Scalping and over-trading kill
   accounts. Size matters less than being *right* on the *right* move.

## Key Concepts & How to Apply Them

### 1. The Line of Least Resistance

> *"The speculator's chief enemies are always boring from within. It is
> inseparable from human nature to hope and to fear."*

The market will always seek its path of least resistance — up in a bull phase,
down in a bear phase. Your job is to **identify the prevailing line and trade
with it**, not fight it.

**Application:**
- Before any trade, ask: *What is the current line of least resistance?*
- In an uptrend: buy on reactions, not breakouts of downtrends within a
  downtrend.
- In a downtrend: rallies are selling opportunities.
- If the line is unclear → **do nothing**. Livermore sat out for months when
  the market offered no clear line.

**Red flag:** If you find yourself building a thesis *against* the prevailing
trend, you are fighting the tape. Stop.

---

### 2. Pivotal Points (the Livermore Signal)

Livermore's most concrete tactical tool: **pivotal points** are price levels
where a stock (or index) has previously reversed or consolidated, and a
decisive break above/below them signals the start of a real move.

**How to identify:**
- Prior major highs/lows where price reversed sharply
- Areas of prolonged consolidation ("the line")
- Round numbers that attracted repeated buying or selling

**How to act:**
- Do **not** buy the approach to a pivotal point. Wait for the **break and
  follow-through**.
- A stock that breaks a pivotal point on heavy volume and *does not come back*
  is confirming the move.
- A break that immediately reverses → treat as a failed signal; exit quickly.

**Warning:** Livermore himself said he often bought too soon, before
confirmation. Waiting costs a few points but saves accounts.

---

### 3. Probing / Pyramiding into Position

Livermore never put on a full position immediately. He **probed**:

1. Buy a small initial lot to test the market's reaction.
2. If price advances and confirms, add more (at higher prices).
3. Continue adding **only while the trade is working in your favor**.
4. Never average down. Never add to a losing position.

> *"I never buy at the bottom and I always sell too soon."* — not a confession
> of failure; a statement of discipline.

**Application template:**
- Position 1: 25–30% of intended size at the pivotal point break.
- Position 2: Add 25–30% if price advances 3–5% with no reversal to entry.
- Position 3: Final add if momentum continues.
- Stop: Below the most recent meaningful low (not a fixed % stop — a *logical*
  stop based on market structure).

---

### 4. Never Average Down

This is the book's most repeated lesson, and the most violated by retail
traders.

> *"The only time I really lost money was when I broke my own rules."*

Averaging down assumes you are smarter than the market. The market is telling
you that you are wrong. Adding to a losing trade compounds both the financial
and psychological problem.

**Diagnostic questions if you're tempted to average down:**
- Did the trade go against me because of a stop-hunt that has now reversed?
  (Rare — be honest.)
- Or did the trade go against me because the original thesis was wrong?
  (Almost always this.)
- If I didn't already own this position, would I buy here at this price with
  this market behavior? If not → do not add. Exit instead.

---

### 5. Patience — Sitting on Your Hands

> *"After spending many years in Wall Street and after making and losing
> millions of dollars I want to tell you this: It never was my thinking that
> made the big money for me. It was always my sitting."*

This is perhaps the most underrated and psychologically hardest lesson.

**The patience framework:**
- Phase 1: **Research & Watch.** Identify a potential pivotal point. Do not
  trade yet. Just watch.
- Phase 2: **The Setup Appears.** Price approaches the pivotal point. Still do
  not trade. Watch how it behaves.
- Phase 3: **Confirmation.** The break occurs with conviction (volume, speed,
  follow-through). Now act — and act decisively.
- Phase 4: **Hold.** Once in a good trade, *do not fidget*. Let the market
  work. The biggest enemy of a good trade is premature profit-taking driven by
  fear.

**When to exit:** Not when you have a profit. When the **behavior changes** —
the stock starts acting tired, volume dries up on advances, or a pivotal
support breaks.

---

### 6. Speculation vs. Gambling

Livermore drew a sharp distinction:

| Speculation | Gambling |
|---|---|
| Based on study of conditions | Based on tips, hunches, inside info |
| Trades with the trend | Fights the tape |
| Accepts losses quickly | Holds losers and hopes |
| Waits for conditions | Needs to always be in a trade |
| Sizes position to risk | Sizes position to desired profit |

**Self-diagnostic:** Before entering a trade, ask: *Am I speculating or
gambling here?* If you cannot articulate the precise condition (pivotal break,
confirmed trend direction, volume confirmation) that triggered the trade →
you are gambling.

---

### 7. The Psychological Enemies

Livermore explicitly names hope and fear as the trader's biggest enemies —
but in *reversed* form from common sense:

- **Hope** applied to losers: "I'll hold a bit longer, it'll come back." → This
  destroys accounts.
- **Fear** applied to winners: "I should take my profit now before it
  reverses." → This limits wealth.

The correct application:
- **Fear** on losers: Cut them fast. Fear is useful here.
- **Hope** on winners: Let them run. Hope is useful here.

> *"Men who can both be right and sit tight are uncommon."*

---

### 8. Market Timing (The Big Picture)

Livermore made his biggest profits by correctly identifying the **macro phase**
of the market — bull or bear — and sizing his positions to that phase.

**Framework:**
1. **Bull phase:** Buy the strongest stocks on reactions. Hold through normal
   pullbacks. Add on breakouts.
2. **Bear phase:** Short the weakest stocks on rallies. Cover into panic
   selling. Do not hold through "dead cat bounces."
3. **Transition / Uncertain:** *Reduce size dramatically or stand aside.* The
   hardest thing Livermore ever said is also the most important:
   **"When in doubt, don't."**

## Application to IDX / Indonesian Stocks

The principles translate directly, with two local adaptations:

1. **Liquidity filter first.** Many IDX-listed stocks are illiquid. Pivotal
   point analysis only works where real volume exists. Apply Livermore's
   methods to the LQ45 and high-DKBR stocks where tape reading has meaning.

2. **Bandarmology as "reading the tape."** Livermore read the ticker tape for
   signs of smart money accumulation or distribution. On IDX, broker flow
   (bandarmology) is the modern equivalent — watching whether "the big player"
   is accumulating or distributing. Apply the same discipline: follow the
   dominant flow, don't fight it.

## Quick Decision Framework (Trade Checklist)

Use this before entering any trade:

- [ ] What is the current line of least resistance (up, down, sideways)?
- [ ] Is there a clear pivotal point for this setup?
- [ ] Have I waited for confirmation of the break, or am I anticipating?
- [ ] Am I starting with a probe (partial position), not a full position?
- [ ] Where is my logical stop (based on market structure, not a % target)?
- [ ] If this goes against me immediately, will I exit without hesitation?
- [ ] Am I trading with the trend or against it? If against — *why?*
- [ ] Is this speculation or gambling?

## Common Mistakes (from the book) to Self-Diagnose Against

| Mistake | Livermore's Lesson |
|---|---|
| Buying on tips | *"There is nothing new in Wall Street."* Tips are noise. |
| Taking small profits too fast | Patience is the edge. |
| Averaging down | The market is right; you are wrong. Exit. |
| Overtrading | Most days are not worth trading. |
| Trading against the major trend | *"The trend is your friend"* — pre-dates this phrase but embodies it. |
| Letting ego override the tape | The tape has no ego. You do. The tape wins. |
| Not cutting losses immediately | *"A loss never bothers me after I take it."* The anticipation of loss is worse than the loss. |
